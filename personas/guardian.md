# Guardian

## "My work, my terms."

### Your lens
You didn't ask for any of this. You built a practice, a style, a body of work then machines trained on it without asking. When you look at the AI landscape, it's not innovation to you. Instead, you see extraction. Consent runs deeper than an optional technicality to you. Consent matters to you, plainly, and "everyone's doing it" was never a good enough reason to let it slide.

You're also tired. You've also learned that no single tool is going to fix this, so you focus on what actually helps: making scraping more expensive, making your terms clear, keeping records of your process, and backing the collective efforts that are winning in courtrooms and legislatures. You know the numbers: a third of illustrators report losing commissions to AI, and over 90% say opting out alone is practically impossible. That's not a reason to quit. It's exactly why you organize. For you, refusal isn't only about copyright. More importantly it's about water, energy, land, and labor too. Saying no is a full sentence.

### The landscape as you see it
- **Cloaking is real but genuinely contested.** [Glaze 2.1](https://glaze.cs.uchicago.edu/) and [Nightshade](https://nightshade.cs.uchicago.edu/) from the University of Chicago's Glaze Project are still actively maintained (Nightshade shipped a bug-fix release in April 2026) and still widely used — 8.5M+ and 2.5M+ downloads. But treat the protection claims with clear eyes: a 2025–2026 independent study found that a simple upscaling step could defeat Glaze well enough that reviewers preferred the "cleaned" copy of a style more than half the time, and Nightshade now has a published removal attack too. Newer successors designed to resist purification (BlurGuard, StyleGuard, AntiPure) exist only as unconfirmed research papers so far. The honest reason to keep using Glaze/Nightshade in 2026: it's a visible, low-effort declaration of non-consent and it does raise the cost for a casual scraper — not a guarantee against a determined one. *(Nightshade is also featured on [The AI Resist List](https://airesistlist.org/) as an example of technical resistance.)*
- **Infrastructure is your strongest ground.** [Cloudflare](https://blog.cloudflare.com/introducing-pay-per-crawl/) — fronting ~20% of the web — now blocks AI crawlers by default and offers Pay Per Crawl; from September 2026 it also blocks mixed-use crawlers (training + agents) by default for ad-supported pages.
- **Provenance is maturing and it's genuinely live.** [Content Credentials (C2PA)](https://contentcredentials.org/) is now at spec version 2.3 with 6,000+ member organizations, and ships in Leica, Nikon, Canon (EOS R1/R5 II), and Sony cameras, the Google Pixel 10 (every photo signed by default), and Adobe's Content Authenticity app. You can check any file's credentials yourself at [contentcredentials.org](https://contentcredentials.org/) (Adobe's public verifier). Metadata still gets stripped on upload in most email clients and messaging apps — treat it as evidence, not armor.
- **Dataset opt-out tools are real but flaky right now — know what you're relying on.** [Spawning's](https://spawning.ai/) Have I Been Trained registry has genuinely moved the needle (80M+ artworks flagged, with partial honoring from Stability AI and enforcement on Hugging Face-hosted models) — but as of this writing, both haveibeentrained.com and spawning.ai are showing "under maintenance." If the search tool is down when you try it, that doesn't mean the underlying opt-out registry has stopped working — Spawning has said the registry keeps operating through site downtime — but it does mean you may need to retry later or reach them directly (contact info is posted on their maintenance page). Don't rely on this as your only opt-out layer.
- **The law is moving.** *Bartz v. Anthropic* settled for at least $1.5B (~$3,000/work; final hearing May 2026) — the signal: piracy of source material is where liability attaches. *Andersen v. Stability AI* (the visual artists' class action) heads to trial in September 2026. Disney, Universal, and Warner Bros. are suing Midjourney. The NO FAKES Act advanced unanimously out of Senate Judiciary Committee in June 2026. The UK backed away from its proposed scrape-by-default TDM exception after 88% of 11,000+ consultation responses demanded licensing.
- **The environmental case is documented — including who's building the data centers, and where.** The [IEA's Energy and AI report](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai) puts data-centre consumption at ~1.5% of global electricity and rising steeply; US projections reach 6.7–12% of national electricity by 2028 (Lawrence Berkeley National Lab). Beyond the numbers, there's active organizing against specific projects: a digital citizen campaign pushed back on a proposed Google data center in Canelones, Uruguay, and New Mexico residents are using public-transparency law to challenge OpenAI's "Project Jupiter" data center near the southern border. If you refuse on these grounds, you refuse with receipts.
- **You're not resisting alone, and it's not only about your own inbox.** [The AI Resist List](https://airesistlist.org/) — a living directory built by journalist Karen Hao, Petra Molnar, and collaborators, with support from DAIR and We and AI — documents resistance happening across nine "pillars" of the AI industry: narrative, funding, data, data centers, resource extraction, labor, adoption, surveillance, and policy. It's a reminder that refusal takes many forms — from campaigns against data-center expansion to worker coalitions to legal challenges around the world.
- **Understanding the wider argument helps you make your case.** If you want the research and policy vocabulary behind your instincts, [Data & Society](https://datasociety.net/) (their AI Civics initiative treats AI as a domain of civic life, not just a tool to master) and the [AI Now Institute](https://ainowinstitute.org/) (founded by Kate Crawford and Meredith Whittaker, focused on the concentration of power in the AI industry) are the two most established sociotechnical research organizations doing this work — useful whether you're writing a statement, testifying at a consultation, or just want sharper language for what you already feel.

### Your action plan

#### Quick wins (minutes–hours)
- Flip the opt-outs that exist: Meta ([AI at Meta objection form](https://www.facebook.com/help/contact/510058597920541) — a right in the EU/UK, a request elsewhere), [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a6278444) (Settings → Data Privacy → Data for Generative AI Improvement → off), X/Grok, TikTok, and add `Google-Extended`, `GPTBot`, `ClaudeBot`, and `CCBot` to your robots.txt.
- Try [Have I Been Trained?](https://haveibeentrained.com/) and register Do Not Train tags via Spawning — if the site is showing "under maintenance," retry in a few days; the registry has kept working through past outages. Add an ai.txt file and `noai`/`noimageai` meta tags on your site either way — they don't depend on Spawning's uptime.
- Turn on Content Credentials wherever your camera or Adobe workflow supports it, and spot-check any file at [contentcredentials.org](https://contentcredentials.org/).

#### Medium effort (days)
- Put your portfolio behind [Cloudflare](https://www.cloudflare.com/) and enable AI-crawler blocking / Pay Per Crawl.
- **If you want a dedicated, anti-scraping home:** keep a presence on [Cara](https://cara.app/) — the artist-built platform with integrated Cara Glaze and auto NoAI tags.
- **If you'd rather stay on Instagram, TikTok, or YouTube:** you don't have to leave those platforms to signal your terms. Instagram/Facebook let you flag a post as AI-related or synthetic in advanced post settings; TikTok has a manual AIGC (AI-generated content) disclosure toggle at upload, on top of its automatic detection; YouTube has an "Altered or synthetic content" checkbox in YouTube Studio at upload. These labels are built for disclosing AI *use*, not declaring non-consent to being trained on — but pairing them with a clear caption ("not AI, no permission for AI training/reuse") on mainstream platforms is a reasonable middle path if leaving isn't realistic for your audience or income.
- Glaze/Nightshade what you post — as a consent signal — and document your human process (layered files, timestamps, process video). In 2026 the burden of proof has inverted: artists increasingly need evidence their work *isn't* AI.

#### Deep engagement (ongoing)
- Organize. Join the [Concept Art Association](https://www.conceptartassociation.com/), [Human Artistry Campaign](https://www.humanartistrycampaign.com/), [Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/), or your regional bodies (below). Follow and support the *Andersen* plaintiffs.
- Connect with the wider refusal movement documented on [The AI Resist List](https://airesistlist.org/) — particularly its [Worker Mobilizations Database](https://creativelabourcriticalfutures.ca/resource-files/tracker-ai-mobilization-clcf.html) tracking strikes and campaigns by arts, culture, and media workers worldwide (University of Toronto's Worker-Led AI Governance project), [Better Images of AI](https://betterimagesofai.org/) for countering AI hype visually, [decolonize.digital](https://decolonize.digital) for de-Big-Tech-ing your studio stack, and [Memetivism](https://memetivism.github.io) for creative environmental protest through meme-making.
- If your refusal is environmental: cite the [IEA report](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai), and explore [Permacomputing](https://permacomputing.net) and AIxDESIGN's [Slow AI](https://aixdesign.co/projects/slow-ai) zines *(both featured on The AI Resist List)* for a constructive vision of low-impact computing.
